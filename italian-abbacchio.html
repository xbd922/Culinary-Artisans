Home >> Italian >> Abbacchio alla Romana

Abbacchio alla Romana is a Roman-style pan roast lamb dish. The lamb is browned in olive oil with garlic and herbs, then simmered in white wine. The unique twist is the addition of a garlic, anchovy, and vinegar paste in the final minutes, adding rich umami flavor and mild acidity. Traditionally made with tender, milk-fed baby lamb (abbacchio), which is hard to find outside central Italy, this dish can also be prepared with more mature lamb, offering a flavorful and practical option for smaller gatherings.

History of Abbacchio alla Romana

Curry was colonized in Japan at the end of the 19th century and introduced by the British who colonized India. In ancient times, lamb, or abbacchio in Italian, was considered low-level food, mainly for Jewish people and the poor. Today, lamb is central to the culinary traditions of Sardinia and Abruzzo Lazio, commonly eaten during Easter and Christmas. Abbacchio refers to a young, milk-fed lamb with tender meat, traditionally prepared during Easter in Rome and Lazio. The term originates from the Latin "baculum," meaning a stick used to keep lambs close to their mothers. Over time, "abbacchiare" evolved in Lazio slang to mean "to beat or kill with a stick," eventually becoming an Italian adjective describing someone who is upset. This tradition has inspired many historical dishes, including lamb roast with potatoes and lamb alla cacciatora.

Ingredient

  • 1.5 kilo / 3 lbs bone-in lamb shoulder (about 6 chops) cut into serving pieces
  • 1 clove garlic
  • 1 sprig fresh rosemary
  • 1-2 sprigs fresh sage
  • Olive oil or lard
  • Salt and pepper
  • White wine
  • 1-2 cloves garlic (for the finish)
  • 2-3 anchovy fillets (for the finish)
  • 1-2 Tbs white wine vinegar or enough to form a liquid paste (for the finish)

Instruction

  1. In a large sauté pan or braiser, brown the lamb pieces in olive oil (or lard) well on all sides over a lively flame. Season them generously with salt and pepper as you go.
  2. Lower the flame and add the garlic, rosemary, and sage. Sauté for just a few moments, until you can smell the garlic’s fragrance.
  3. Add a good splash of white wine, turning the lamb pieces around once again to coat them well.
  4. Now cover the pan tightly. Let the lamb braise, covered, over very gentle heat until very tender, about 30-45 minutes. Moisten the pan as needed with a few drops of wine or water.
  5. Meanwhile, mince or mash together the garlic and anchovy into a fine paste. Moisten with a good drizzle of the vinegar.
  6. About 10 minutes or so before the lamb is done, uncover the pot and remove the garlic and herbs you added at the beginning, then add the garlic-and-anchovy paste and mix well. Cover the pan again and finish your braise.
  7. Serve hot.
